01 / THE PROBLEM

Zoom out

A third of the world's disease burden requires surgery.

Samalani's hernia repair is one of the most common operations in medicine. What is rare, where he lives, is getting one. About a third of the global burden of disease requires surgical or anesthesia care, but the poorest third of the world receives only about 6% of the world's operations. The good news is that this is one of the most fixable problems in global health.

02 / THE COST

Financial catastrophe

Paying for surgery pushes 81 million people a year into financial catastrophe.

For Samalani's family, $575 is not a painful bill. It is an impossible one. Health economists call it catastrophic expenditure: a medical bill that costs more than 10% of a family's income. An estimated 3.7 billion people would face costs like that if they needed surgery today. The five patients on this page live in the countries where that risk is highest. For perspective: if you earn at least $47,000 a year after tax, you are in the richest 2% of people in the world. Most of us underestimate how much power that gives us to help.

03 / THE GAP

The three gaps

Why the cost falls on families.

In the countries where these five patients live, health systems have very little to spend, most care is paid for in cash, and surgeons are scarce. All three show up clearly in the data.

Low-income countries~43%

Without insurance, families pay the hospital directly, in cash, at the time of care. The WHO considers the risk of catastrophic spending low only when out-of-pocket payments fall below 15–20% of a country's total health spending.

The surgeon gap

Not a single low-income country meets the target of 20 specialists per 100,000. In West Ethiopia, there is no ENT surgeon within roughly 800 km of Mekdes's region. A typical patient waits two to three months just to meet one.
